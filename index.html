Sketch narrative

This sketch represents the rhythm of box breathing, a meditation technique used to calm the mind and reduce stress by maintaining a steady 4-4-4-4 breathing cycle: breathe in, hold, breathe out, hold. It is designed for mindfulness practice, stress management sessions, and focus exercises where users need visual and temporal guidance to regulate their breathing.

  • Design decisions:
    1. Square path visualization: The breathing cycle is represented by a square with four edges, where each edge corresponds to one breathing phase. A colored line progressively draws around the square, providing clear visual tracking of the 4-second intervals. The square shape reinforces the "box breathing" concept .
    2. Color-coded phases: Each of the four phases uses a distinct teal-to-gray gradient color (teal for inhale, light blue for first hold, mint for exhale, gray for second hold).
    3. Countdown timer with neon glow: Large text displays the current phase label.The neon glow effect on the lines creates a calming, modern aesthetic while maintaining high visibility against the starry background.
  • Future work: Add customizable breathing durations (e.g., 3-3-3-3 or 5-5-5-5 cycles) to accommodate different skill levels/ Include audio cues or gentle sound at phase transitions to support eyes-closed practice.

Sketch narrative

This lotus clock is designed for meditation sessions where users want to visualize time passing in a gentle, nature-inspired way. Each petal represents one minute, creating a tangible sense of time's passage without the anxiety of watching traditional clock hands. Perfect for timed meditation practices, tea ceremonies, or mindful study sessions.

  • Design decisions:
    1. 60-petal system: Each of the 60 petals represents one minute, disappearing clockwise from the top. This creates an intuitive countdown that shows both elapsed time and remaining time in the hour, helping users stay aware without breaking meditative focus.
    2. Falling petal animation: When a petal disappears, it falls with rotation and fade effects, transforming the "loss" of time into a beautiful, zen moment.
    3. Moon clock for hours: Instead of another circular element, the moon in the upper right displays hours with 12 marks and a smooth-moving hand. This separates hour and minute information clearly while maintaining the celestial/nature theme.
  • Future work: Add optional chime sound when petals fall for audio-visual meditation sync/ Create a "reverse mode" where petals grow back instead of fall, for timing breaks between meditation sessions.

Sketch narrative

This breathing moon clock is designed for meditation practitioners who need visual guidance for breathing exercises. It provides a calming, rhythmic visual that helps synchronize breathing patterns during mindfulness practice.

  • Design decisions:
    1. Breathing rhythm visualization: The moon expands during inhale (4s) and contracts during exhale (4s), creating an intuitive visual guide that eliminates the need to count. The easing function ensures smooth, organic movement that feels natural to follow.
    2. Moon phase as time indicator: Instead of traditional clock hands, the moon's phase represents minutes (0-60), completing a full lunar cycle each hour. This reinforces the meditation theme while providing time awareness without being distracting.
    3. Interactive breathing speeds: Clicking the moon cycles through three breathing patterns—Normal (4-4s), Deep (6-6s), and Quick (3-3s)—allowing users to adjust the pace based on their meditation practice level or current needs.
  • Future work: Add sound cues (gentle bell or chime) at breathing transitions to support eyes-closed meditation/ Include a session timer that counts completed breathing cycles for practice tracking.

Design Rationale

Author-Driven vs User-Driven

This visualization is primarily author-driven with light user-driven elements.

Author-Driven aspects:

  • The calendar layout guides users chronologically through the year (Jan→Dec), telling a clear story: "when to fly and when to avoid"
  • The visualization highlights the WORST DAY (Dec 23) and BEST DAY (Feb 29) upfront, directing attention to key findings
  • Red/yellow/green color encoding immediately communicates good vs bad days without user interpretation needed

User-Driven aspects:

  • Users can hover over specific dates to explore detailed information (delay rate, average delay time, years included)
  • Users can find their preferred travel dates and check the delay risk interactively
